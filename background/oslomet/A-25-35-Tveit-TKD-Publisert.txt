Abstract
The research presented in this thesis are related to the structural
analyses of a floating photovoltaic (FPV) concept developed by Sunlit
Sea AS. They use a metallic buoyant float structure that acts as a
thermal bridge between the water body and the photovoltaic panels. For
the solution to be commercially viable, the structure must be efficient in
terms of heat transportation and material use. This has motivated the
company to explore innovative structural designs, where the current
solution is a shallow-draft shell structure made up of two formed
aluminum alloy sheet components. As the platform must maintain its
structural integrity throughout the intended lifetime, fatigue failure due to
ocean wave-induced wave loads is a concern.
This thesis presents new models and approaches for the analysis of
such structures. One of the key contributions is a submodeling technique
that incorporates sheet metal forming (SMF) effects in the structural
analysis of formed components. For a critical component region, sheet
metal forming simulations are conducted in LS-DYNA to obtain realistic
geometric configurations and distributions of residual stresses and
material thinning. Then, a simplified service load analysis of the
complete structure is performed to obtain boundary conditions for the
LS-DYNA model by using a mapping technique based on normalized
segment length interpolation. It is shown how a continuum mechanicsbased fatigue model can be combined with SMF submodeling to
estimate the structure’s fatigue life under high-cycle loading.
It is revealed that the continuum mechanics fatigue model cannot
correctly predict shear stress-dominated fatigue failure for materials that
display brittle or very ductile fatigue behavior. This results in two
proposed modifications to enhance the model’s efficacy. The first
modification adopts a Hershey-Hosford function to generalize the
endurance surface formulation. This solves the issue for isotropic ductile
metallic materials where the relationship between the fatigue limits in
fully reversed shear stress and fully reversed normal stress varies from
1/2 to 0.585. The second modification proposes a conditionally convex

between the shear and normal stress fatigue limits by considering the
effect of the Lode angle. This is of significant interest for exploring float
designs based on additive manufacturing, since these materials
reportedly display highly brittle fatigue behavior.

Introduction

1 Introduction
Context and motivation
The utilization of new global surface areas is one of the main reasons
why floating photovoltaics (FPV) are multiplying [1]. So far, the
technology has been developed for use in lakes and other freshwater
reservoirs. As 65% of the world's largest cities are situated near the
coast [2,3], adaptation to marine environments opens new possibilities
for upscaling, especially in countries with competing interests for
available land due to high population densities. Thus, FPVs can
contribute significantly to locally produced renewable energy, avoiding
the area conflicts increasingly seen in land-based solar and wind
power [4-6].
The FPV technology developer Sunlit Sea AS delivers a system of
prefabricated floating solar installations designed to handle freshwater
as well as in-shore marine environment. The system comprises several
interconnected FPV units that are kept in position by a mooring system,
see Figure 1.1.a). The matrix of FPV units can be scaled to meet the
desired production capacity of the customer. Each unit consists of a
photovoltaic (PV) panel that is directly attached to a low profile, metallic
float structure that provides buoyancy while acting as a thermal gateway
to transport heat from the solar panels into the cool seawater. This
allows the PVs to operate at lower temperatures, which increases the
solar-cell efficiency [7,8]. For the technology to reach its potential, the
design must be efficient with respect to heat transfer and material costs.
These challenges have motivated the developer to look into innovative
designs and production techniques for the metallic float structure. In the
current design, each FPV unit, approximately 2 x 2 m in size, consists of
two formed aluminum alloy sheet components. The components are
joined together, back-to-back, and sealed along the edges to form a
hollow shell structure, see Figure 1.1.b). With its indented cups, the
concept was inspired by honeycomb sandwich structures, which are

widely applied as lightweight rigid structures in the aerospace
industry [9,10].
The cups provide structural rigidity against out-of-plane loading and
creates a thermal bridge through the hollow structure, see Figure 1.2.a).
For the design that is currently in production, Sunlit Sea’s collaborative
partner Accura Engineering has developed a cupcake tray-like shape
that is feasible to manufacture by deep-drawing sheets of 1.5 mm thick
AA5083-H111 close to their forming limits. The component is illustrated
in Figure 1.2.b). While more cups would enhance the cooling effect, the
aluminum alloy sheets must remain intact during forming of the
components.

Aggressively pushing the material’s drawing limits can lead to increased
failure rates in the production line. Avoiding this requires a good
understanding of the material’s mechanical behavior and the various
parameters that control the sheet metal forming process. Additionally,
the sheet metal forming may influence the structure’s properties through
the introduction of residual stresses [11,12], plastic hardening [13],
material thinning, and geometric deviations. Such effects should be
accounted for when assessing the structural capacity towards the limit
states specified by the recommended practices for the design and
development of floating photovoltaic systems, e.g. DNV-RP-0548 [14].
The structures must be designed to handle potential extreme events
accounted for in the ultimate limit state (ULS). Moreover, repeated stress
can cause high-cycle fatigue (HCF) failure due to stresses well below
the material’s static capacity [15]. Thus, the design must also consider
the fatigue limit state (FLS) to ensure that the structure endures the
design life.
Expanding FPV technologies from freshwaters to marine environments
requires operating in deeper waters and higher wind velocities. As a
result, the structures must handle increased hydrodynamic
loading [16,17]. The total wave load is the combined effects of wind-sea
and swell waves [18]. Swell waves are unidirectional waves of larger
wave lengths that are unaffected by local weather systems. Wind-sea
waves are chaotic short-crested waves and their directionality is spread
about the local wind direction [19], independently of the direction of the
swell waves [20]. Thus, the fatigue analysis of the marine FPV structure
should account for loading due to waves of varying amplitudes and
frequencies attacking from different directions simultaneously. Such
loading can induce complex multiaxial stress histories where the stress
components vary independently of each other.
In multiaxial HCF analysis the traditional approach relies on converting
stress histories into a finite set of cycles using cycle-counting
techniques, see e.g. ref. [21]. The treatment of arbitrary multiaxial stress
histories remains a challenge for this methodology due to its intrinsic
assumption that any stress history consists of distinct cycles. This issue

has spurred a branch of alternative fatigue models where the stress
history dictates the evolution of the fatigue damage in an approach
consistent with principles from continuum mechanics [22]. This modeling
appraoch shows promise, but its novelty implies the requirement of
further development. For instance, the model can make inaccurate
fatigue predictions for brittle and very ductile materials, and for certain
stress histories [23,24].

Objectives and scope
The thesis aims to enhance the understanding of the high-cycle fatigue
capacity of formed structural components subjected to complex load
histories. The study seeks to advance and improve existing
methodologies for sheet metal forming and fatigue analysis that can be
used as parts of a comprehensive structural analysis process.
The objectives of the current study are to:
• explore how numerical sheet metal forming analysis can be used
for the development and design of new products;
• gain a deeper understanding of how the high-cycle fatigue capacity
of formed structures are affected by the sheet metal forminginduced plastic deformations;
• develop and advance analysis methods for integrating sheet metal
forming effects in the fatigue assessment of formed components,
aiming to inspire industrial players to imporve current practices;
and
• investigate and improve state-of-the-art theories for predicting
high-cycle fatigue failure induced by complex load histories.
Although the study is largely motivated by the engineering challenges
faced by Sunlit Sea AS, it strives to obtain generalizable knowledge with
relevance beyond specific types of structures or alloys. Thus, its purpose
is not to validate the proposed FPV concept with respect to the fatigue
limit state. Nor is the objective to perform a comprehensive fatigue

Introduction

assessment to account for all relevant effects. Such an interdisciplinary
exercise would involve several research topics that are outside the
scope of this thesis.
For instance, obtaining realistic scenarios for the loads induced by
waves, winds and currents requires a good understanding of the FPV
system’s hydrodynamic behavior. The loads experienced by the float
structures heavily depend on the size of the matrix (cf. Figure 1.1.a)), the
properties of the connective hinges that transfer loads between the FPV
units [25], the local metocean conditions, and the presence of highfrequency waves [25]. Such analyses will not be discussed in this thesis.
In addition to sheet metal forming, the fatigue capacity of the floats may
be influenced by other manufacturing effects. Currently, the two
aluminum alloy components that make up the structure are joined using
friction stir welding or clinching. Challenges remain in accurately
estimating the static strength and fatigue life of such
connections [26-30]. The fatigue capacity may also be affected by the
presence of chlorides in the marine environment, which can cause
fatigue cracks to initiate in corrosion pits on the surface of the aluminum
sheets [31-33]. This work does not consider these aspects.

Research questions
The research questions of this thesis aim to guide the study in directions
that align with the objectives and scope in Section 1.2, by addressing
open questions and gaps in the literature.
R1:

How do parameters related to the sheet metal forming process
influence the drawing limits, and how can the dependencies
between different parameters be explored efficiently?

R2:

How and to what extent does sheet metal forming influence the
operational stress levels in formed components, and can such
effects be accounted for in the fatigue assessments of structures?

Research strategy
The activities that constitute the work in this thesis consist of four main
components. An overview is provided Figure 1.3.
The first component is a literature study that provides an overview of
relevant theory and state-of-the-art methodologies for sheet metal
forming and fatigue analysis. It identifies research gaps and directions
for further research. The work is presented in Section 2, and in the
respective introduction-sections of this thesis’ publications.
The second task involves the experimental characterization of the plastic
behavior of the AA5083-H111 sheets. This work provides data that is be
used to calibrate anisotropic yield criteria and strain hardening functions
for use in finite element-based numerical analyses in LS-DYNA. Details
about this campaign are provided in Publication III.
The third activity is finite element-based analyses of the sheet metal
forming process, and the structure in operation in LS-DYNA. The
purpose of this work is to assist the design development process of the
FPV solution (Publication I), and to investigate the effects that the sheet
metal forming process has on the operational stresses and the fatigue
capacity (Publications II and III). Specific information about these
investigations are found in the respective publications.
The fourth and last component is the application and further
development of an existing high-cycle fatigue model suitable for analysis
of multiaxial arbitrary stress histories. In Publication II it is demonstrated
how the model can be used in combinations with sheet metal forming
analysis to account for forming effects in the fatigue analysis of the FPV

Basic theory
This thesis consistently refers to right-handed Cartesian coordinate
systems with orthogonal axes denoted as 𝑥, 𝑦, and 𝑧. Mainly three types
of coordinate systems are encountered. The fatigue analyses in
Publications IV, and V refer to stress states on cylindrical or tubular test
specimens where 𝑥 and 𝑦 respectfully align with in the specimen’s
longitudinal and circumferential directions, while 𝑧 is normal to the
material’s surface. The finite element analyses in Publications I, II, and
III employ a global coordinate system where 𝑧 is the upward direction,
and 𝑥 and 𝑦 align with the square FPV float’s edges. These models also
employ co-rotational coordinate systems that rotate with the
material [34,35]. Throughout the current section, 𝑥, 𝑦, and 𝑧 will refer to
the co-rotational material coordinates.
Vectors and tensors are referred to using bold font, e.g. 𝒙 represents the
position of a particle with the coordinates 𝑥, 𝑦, and 𝑧. When it is
convenient to adopt a numbered form, the notations 𝑥1 , 𝑥2 , and 𝑥3 are
taken as synonymous with 𝑥, 𝑦, and 𝑧. Lower case Latin letters will be
used for short forms, e.g. 𝑥𝑖 , where 𝑖 = 1, 2, 3, represent the components
of 𝒙. Moreover, we adopt the Einstein summation convention, where if
an index appears twice within a term, it implies a summation over that
index. For example, 𝐴𝑖𝑖 = 𝐴11 + 𝐴22 + 𝐴33 , where 𝑖 = 1, 2, 3, is a dummy
index, meaning its label is arbitrary (see e.g. [36]). Exceptions occur
when the letters directly refer to the Cartesian axes, i.e. 𝑥𝑥, 𝑦𝑦, and 𝑧𝑧,
in which case summation is not implied.

model. Not only does it define the onset of yielding for triaxial stress

Until now, we have considered isotropic formulations which utilize
principal stresses or stress tensor invariants to describe the yield
surface. However, many materials display mechanical properties that are
dependent on the material direction. This is the case for rolled aluminum

linear transformation-based stress components.

High-cycle fatigue
The fatigue failure of a structural component is the ultimate destiny of a
material that has gone through various consecutive stages, each
governed by different physical phenomena. A structural component that
is subjected to cyclic stresses over a certain threshold level may
experience microscopic cracking on the free surface of the material. If
these microscopic cracks are allowed to accumulate through repeated
stress cycles, a macroscopic crack eventually forms. The tip of the
macroscopic crack gives rise to a stress concentration that causes the
crack to propagate in a stable manner through the material. This

29

Background and theoretical framework

gradually reduces the cross-section and leads to an increase in the
nominal stress levels. Eventually, the ultimate capacity of the material is
reached, at which the component fails in a sudden and often
catastrophic event.
When designing structures to avoid high-cycle fatigue failure, several
approaches exist. The engineer may design the structure so that the
magnitude of the cyclic stress does not exceed the material’s fatigue
limit. The stress level at which a material can endure an infinite (or very
large) number of cycles without failing due to fatigue is called the fatigue
limit. While such a strategy may provide confidence in the structure’s
integrity, it can result in overly conservative designs and non-optimal
resource allocations.
A second approach is more refined, and allows some formation of
microcracks during the structure’s service life. In this case, stress levels
that exceed the material’s fatigue limit are tolerated, as long as
macroscopic crack formation is avoided. While this allows utilizing more
of the structure’s capacity, it requires increased knowledge about the
material, and how it behaves under various loading conditions.
It is also possible to consider the total fatigue life of the structure. By
allowing the formation and growth of macroscopic cracks, the full
capacity of the structure can be utilized, as long as the structure is taken
out of service prior to total collapse. The modeling of fatigue crack
propagation requires specific information about how the material
behaves in the crack propagation phase.
As the majority of the fatigue life is often spent in the crack initiation
phase, this thesis will focus on the first two approaches. It will be
conservatively assumed that the number of cycles required to initiate a
macroscopic fatigue crack defines the fatigue life of a structure.
Moreover, the scope is limited to fatigue due to local stress histories,
disregarding effects related to specimen size, surface roughness,
temperature and chemical environments.

2.3.1 S-N curve
Research on fatigue started in the middle of the 19th century with the
experiments of August Wöhler. He carried out the first organized studies
on materials that failed under constant harmonic loading at stress
amplitudes far below the material’s static strength [58]. When studying
the fatigue of railroad axles, Wöhler observed an inverse relationship
between the applied stress range, and the number of cycles at failure.
The most common experimental fatigue test is that of uniaxial stress,
which is achieved by subjecting test specimens to repeated bending or
axial loading. If the specimen is aligned with the 𝑥-direction, 𝜎𝑥𝑥 = 𝜎 is
the only non-zero stress components, and the stress history is defined

assuming that the angular frequency 𝜔 and the time 𝑡 does not influence
result, the fatigue stress can simply be written as 𝜎 = 𝜎𝑚 ± 𝜎𝑎 .
The results from such tests are typically represented in an S-N diagram
(often referred to as a Wöhler’s diagram) with the stress amplitude or
range (2𝜎𝑎 ) on the vertical axis and the number of cycles at failure on
the horizontal axis. Figure 2.6 displays a schematic S-N diagram in the
log-log space of the stress amplitude 𝜎𝑎 , and the number of cycles at
failure 𝑁𝑓 . The diagram is divided in three domains; the low-cycle, the

straight line in the log-log-space.
The low-cycle fatigue (LCF) domain is found from > 1 to around 103 -104
cycles at failure. At these stress levels, the material normally
experiences macroscopic plastic deformations. The high-cycle (HCF)
fatigue domain is found from around 103 -104 to around 106 -107 cycles at
failure. While microscopic plastic deformations can occur, the

more computational expensive, they are often regarded as more
accurate.

2.3.4 Non-periodic stress
Usually, variable amplitude stress histories due to e.g. irregular wave
and wind loads [94-96] are tackled by applying cycle-counting

37

Background and theoretical framework

techniques. This involves first converting the stress tensor history into a
scalar variable by using an effective stress function. Next, this
continuous signal is filtered into a sequence of valley and peaks. A cyclecounting technique then converts the sequence into a finite set of distinct
equivalent cycles. The fatigue damage associated with each stress cycle
is calculated using S-N curves, which relate the stress amplitude and the
associated number of cycles at failure. Finally, the total damage is
calculated from the contributions of each cycle by applying a damageaccumulation rule. As cycle-counting techniques often rely on making
alterations to the stress sequence that introduce additional cycles to the
analysis, the linear Palmer-Miner accumulation rule [97,98], which
disregards sequence effects, must be used.
Numerous cycle-counting techniques exist, where different variations of
the rain-flow technique by Matsuishi & Endo [21] are the most commonly
applied. The Bannantine-Socie method [99], the Wang-Brown
method [100,101] and the modified Wang-Brown method proposed by
Meggiolaro & Castro [87] represent some prominent examples. Rovinelli
et al. [102] compared four rain-flow-based cycle-counting techniques for
multiaxial loading and concluded that the predicted damage among the
different approaches varied up to 20%. Carpinteri et al. [103] reviewed
the state-of-the-art of multiaxial fatigue modeling for non-periodic loading
and highlighted the complex nature of the analysis methods, which
combine various equivalent stress formulations, critical plane-definitions,
and cycle-counting techniques. Although cycle-counting-based methods
may be appropriately applied for many engineering applications, the
authors of the study concluded that the existing approaches all reveal
various deficiencies. Thus, there is no consensus on a generally valid
theory for arbitrary stress histories.
Other authors have pointed to the fallacy in the assumption that all
stress histories consist of well-defined cycles [22]. In principle, the
stresses in a structure are described by six stress tensor components
that are independent of each other. Thus, their stress reversals do not
necessarily occur simultaneously [104]. When they do not, the methods

38

Background and theoretical framework

fail to define equivalent cycles without the potential loss of important
information [105].
The practical shortcomings of employing cycle-counting-based methods
in combination with large FE models were discussed by Lilja et al. [106].
As the cycle-counting algorithms require access to the entire stress
history, the fatigue analysis must be carried out as a post-processing
step. To determine the maxima and minima of non-proportional stress
histories with satisfying accuracy requires high-frequency stress output.
If the FE model consists of a large number of integration points, the
amount of stored data can become substantial.

2.3.5 Continuum mechanics-based fatigue
modeling
Lately, an increased interest has emerged for incremental fatigue
models. These models are based on time-continuous formulations where
the fatigue damage is assumed to accumulate continuously, opposed to
in the cycle-based formulations discussed before.
Stephanov [107] proposed a model for multiaxial fatigue analysis where
the fatigue damage was continuously integrated along stress
trajectories. The model allowed evaluating fatigue due to arbitrary
histories of plane stress.
Ottosen et al. [22] proposed an incremental theory that accounted for all
six stress tensor components. The model, here referred to as the
continuum mechanics fatigue model, uses the concept of an endurance
surface that moves in the stress space according to a deviatoric backstress tensor. The inside of the surface is defined as the safe domain,
and the outside of the surface is defined as the unsafe domain. In the
unsafe domain, the fatigue damage may evolve if the stress point is
moving away from the surface. The same conditions apply for the
evolution of the back-stress tensor. Unlike cycle-counting-based
methods, which require access to entire stress histories, the continuum
mechanics fatigue model employ the current stress tensor 𝝈 and its

Since its publication in 2008, the continuum mechanics fatigue model
has received attention in the scientific community as a promising
framework for fatigue analysis. It has been used in statistical fatigue
analyses [110,111], in studies on topology optimization [112-115], and in
the fatigue assessments of various structures and
materials [24,106,108,109,116-120]. Moreover, several generalizations
have been proposed to expand its applicability. This includes the
consideration of notch effects [121-124], sequence effects [125],

[108,128,129], ductility [130], and brittleness [131].

combinations of shear stress and normal stress [23,106,109], including
Publication IV of this thesis. The issue arises for stress histories defined

Another limitation of the model has been related to its predictions for
shear-dominated stress histories in very ductile or brittle materials.
Because the continuum mechanics fatigue model reduces to the Sines
criterion under periodic linear loading, it assumes that the relationship
𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 = 1⁄√3 holds for all materials, cf. Eqs. (2.47) and (2.48).
However, experimental data demonstrate that 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 can vary
significantly depending on the material’s ductility or brittleness (cf. the
discussion in Section 2.3.3). When calibrated to uniaxial fatigue test
data, the model is thus prone to non-conservative predictions for

materials with 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 ≤ 1⁄√3, and overly conservative predictions for
materials where 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 significantly exceeds 1⁄√3.
Barriere et al. [117] attempted to rectify this constraint when modelling
fatigue in a glassy polymer. They proposed to scale the off-diagonal
components of the stress tensor matrix. While this provides a solution
applicable in the specific reference frame, it makes the material behavior
dependent on the orientation of the applied coordinate system. Thus, the
approach violates material objectivity.
Publication IV of this thesis proposes a generalization for ductile
materials that allows the ratio 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 to take on values from 1⁄2 to
0.585. This is done by adopting a Hershey-Hosford function, cf.
Eq. (2.25), to replace the original von Mises-like back-stress-modified
effective stress term in the endurance surface formulation. As the
expression is written in terms of the principal stress components,
material objectivity is satisfied.
Modeling the behavior of brittle materials where 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 > 2/3 requires
the use of a non-convex effective stress term [133]. In Publication V of
this thesis, it is shown that the endurance surface can take on nonconvex configurations without violating the requirements placed by the
second law of thermodynamics. This opens for a variety of possible
endurance surface formulations. The publication presents a generalized
form written in terms of stress tensor invariants that can take on arbitrary
𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 -ratios.

Sheet metal forming and fatigue
In their current design, Sunlit Sea utilize a metallic FPV structure that
consists of two aluminum alloy sheet components that are manufactured
through sheet metal forming. This technique is widely applied across
various industries due to its ability to efficiently produce durable,
lightweight components in high volumes. Formed components are used
in for a range of applications, although the technological development
has largely been driven by the automotive industry.

46

Background and theoretical framework

In conventional sheet metal forming processes, thin metal sheets are
plastically deformed into desired geometric shapes through mechanical
interactions with forming tools. In this process, the flat metal blank is
punched into a die shape, forcing the blank to take on its form. Blank
holders are used to keep the surrounding areas in place, preventing the
sheet material from wrinkling. During forming, the sheets are subjected
to large plastic and small elastic deformations. After the force from the
forming tools are released, the formed component experiences
springback due to the elastic unloading.
Developing the design of a formed component involves designing a
forming process that allows the component to be produced without
defects. As the sheets are subjected to large strains, a sequence of
instability phenomena can occur. It often starts with diffuse necking,
followed by localized necking and/or shear instability, and ends with
ductile failure due to the coalescence of voids in the material [134].
Thus, a vital part of the design is to avoid the onset of local necking or
shear localization. In the last decades, the tool making industry has
gradually replaced expensive trial-and-error procedures with computeraided FE analysis [135,136]. FE-based analysis models are then
equipped with calibrated material models to simulate the sheet metal
forming process [51,137,138], or to predict the forming limits of
materials [134,139-141].
Although these numerical tools are mainly used to analyze formability in
production, they may also be used to study the functionality of
components. The sheet metal forming process induces a series of
effects that influence the mechanical behavior of the formed
components. The integration of such effects is essential for realistically
predicting their fatigue life [142].
For instance, the fatigue capacity may be influenced by the plastic
straining. Experimental studies have shown that the increased
dislocation density associated with high plastic strains often increases
the fatigue strength of materials [143]. Several authors have studied the
effect for various steels used for sheet metal forming applications. For

47

Background and theoretical framework

instance, Gustavsson et al. [144] tested pre-strained and virgin steel
sheets, and reported that the fatigue strength of the virgin sheets were
about 15-30% lower than in the pre-strained sheets. Moreover, Zhao et
al. [143] performed tests on SAPH440 steel sheets and reported that the
pre-strain had a beneficial effect of the fatigue strength, and that the
effect was mostly pronounced in the low-cycle domain.
However, there are also reports of materials where the fatigue strength
is reduced due to plastic pre-strain. For instance, Froustey &
Lataillade [145] tested two different aluminium alloy sheets and found
that one alloy was insensitive to the pre-strain (AA5454-O), while the
other experienced a pronounced decrease in the fatigue strength
(A2017A-T3). Similar results were obtained by Lanning et al. [146] who
saw that the pre-strain had a detrimental effect on the high-cycle fatigue
properties of a titanium alloy. Kang et al. [147] also reported that the
fatigue strength of a carbon steel was reduced when subjected to high
pre-strains, likely due to pre-crack formation. Moreover, Das et al. [148]
found that the pre-strain effect was path-dependent, showcasing the
complexity of the relationship between plastic pre-straining and fatigue
strength.
When the sheet material is subjected to tensile plastic deformations in
the planar directions, the material experiences thinning. The reduced
sheet thickness implies that the stress levels must rise in order for the
cross-section to carry the same load. Several studies have reported that
thinning due to plastic deformation negatively impacts the fatigue
capacity of structures [13,149-151].
During the forming process, elastic strains are stored in the component.
This causes springback of the component upon release of the forming
tools, see Figure 2.9. Although springback can be accounted for in the
design of forming tools, some geometric deviations between the desired
shape and the actual component occur in practice. Since the
component’s shape influences how stresses from external loads
distribute, these deviations between the real components and the

In the formed component, inhomogeneous distributions of plastic strains
prevent some elastic strains from returning to the stress-free state. This
gives rise to residual stresses [153], which also have an impact on
fatigue [142]. The residual stresses are often opposite to the stresses
acting at the end of the forming; the elongated side of a radius often
experience compressive residual stresses, and the compressed side
typically sees tensile residual stresses [154]. These act as superimposed
mean stresses and can thus significantly influence the fatigue capacity
of the component (cf. Section 2.3.2).
Nevertheless, it has been ordinary practice to analyze the fatigue life of
formed automotive components using idealized models that do not
explicitly account for any of these effects [150]. It has been a common
perception that the negative effects of sheet thinning are cancelled by
the positive effects of plastic strains [151], but as demonstrated by e.g.
Froustey & Lataillade [145], this cannot be appropriately assumed for all
materials. The effects of residual stresses are simply disregarded,
despite that the importance of accounting for such effects in fatigue
analysis is widely recognized in the research community [155-157].
There are few studies that combine forming simulations and fatigue
analysis, and the ones that do are often smaller investigations

49

Background and theoretical framework

conducted by industry players in the automotive sector, see
refs. [149-152].
Dannbauer et al. [150] integrated the effects of plastic straining and
sheet thinning in the fatigue analysis of a deep-drawn steering knuckle
made from ZStE380 steel sheets. The sheet metal forming analysis was
performed in a specialized software, and the results of sheet thinning
and plastic strains were mapped to a simplified ABAQUS model that was
used to obtain operational stresses for fatigue analysis. To account for
the effect of plastic straining, the authors adopted two different
approaches (proposed by Masendorf [158] and Hatscher [159], as cited
in [150]) where the effective plastic strain always has a favorable effect
on the fatigue life. While this assumption may have been appropriate for
the material subject to investigation, these methods would not be
capable of capturing the adverse effects reported by Froustey &
Lataillade [145] and Kang et al. [147].
Facchinetti et al. [149] considered forming effects in the fatigue life
assessment of a suspension arm made from S460MC steel sheets.
Thickness variations, plastic strains and residual stresses were obtained
from forming simulations, and the results were mapped onto a simplified
ABAQUS model where the service stress analysis was performed. The
critical plane-based Dang-Van model [81] was employed for a multiaxial
fatigue analysis. Plastic strain effects were accounted for by an
approach similar to that of Masendorf [158], as cited in Dannbauer et
al. [150], which assumes that plastic strains always improves the fatigue
strength. Moreover, a simple von Mises yield function with linear strain
hardening was used for the forming analysis, which raises questions
about the accuracy of the forming predictions.
Kose & Rietmann [152] investigated the fatigue performance of a
chassis part by including the effects of forming and joining. Sheet
thinning, plastic strain-effects, and residual stresses were accounted for,
but the methods that were used to obtain the predictions were not
presented. Nevertheless, the results from these process simulations
were mapped to a simplified model for fatigue analysis, where the

50

Background and theoretical framework

integration points of the new mesh adopted the state variable of the
nearest integration points in the old mesh. The authors concluded that
the fatigue capacity of the specific component was improved, but did not
discuss the different effects separately. Moreover, they highlighted the
limitation of not accounting for the geometric effects of springback,
particularly in combination with the applied mapping technique.
Hariharan & Prakash [142] reviewed scenarios and challenges related to
the integration of forming effects in fatigue life predictions, as of 2012.
They discussed the importance of employing advanced constitutive
models to obtain realistic predictions of the state variables that affect the
fatigue lives of formed components. They further emphasized that the
accuracy of the sheet metal forming predictions is dependent on the
constitutive models. In particular, it was argued that appropriate yield
criteria and hardening rules that can account for mixed isotropickinematic behavior should be employed. Yet, to the author’s knowledge,
no studies have performed high-cycle fatigue analysis where the sheet
metal forming effects are accounted for with sophisticated anisotropic
plasticity models.
The challenges of mapping results from finely meshed forming
simulation models to more coarsely meshed fatigue models were also
discussed in the review [142]. In forming simulations, adaptive meshing
is commonly used to capture the details of geometric features. This often
results in large analysis models that are impractical for use in fatigue
analysis. Limitations related to mapping algorithms could be avoided by
analyzing sheet metal forming effects locally, and not for the whole
structure. Then, submodelling could be used to perform a service load
analysis for the local region. Boundary conditions for the local submodel
could be obtained from a simplified global model established from the
idealized CAD geometry of the component. Such an approach requires a
robust submodeling technique that can deal with the lack of geometric
compatibility that arises due to springback-induced geometric
imperfections. This issue is addressed in Publication III of this thesis.
Here, the conventional shell-to-shell mapping technique is replaced by
interpolation along normalized submodel boundary segment lengths.

Publication IV
In this work, we propose a generalization of the continuum mechanics
fatigue model by Ottosen et al. [22] to account for the 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 -ratios
observed in very ductile materials. Experiments show that the ratio may
approach 1⁄2 for very ductile materials, while the original continuum
mechanics fatigue model employs a formulation where the ratio is
always 1⁄√3. When fatigue data based on uniaxial loading is used for
calibration, it follows that the original model makes non-conservative
predictions of shear stress fatigue in materials where 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 < 1⁄√3.
To rectify this, we replace the von Mises-like back-stress-reduced
effective stress function in the original model by the high-exponent
Hershey-Hosford-formulation (cf. Section 2.2.2). The new formulation
allows the model to take on 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 -ratios ranging from 1⁄2 to 0.585 by
calibrating the exponential parameter of the Hershey-Hosford function.
We systematically validate the proposed model towards experimental
multiaxial fatigue limit data from eight different materials with 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1
ranging from 0.5 to 0.6. Application of the modified formulation results in
an overall better prediction accuracy. The largest improvement is seen
for a NiCrMo 75/80-ton steel where 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 = 0.519. For fully reversed
torsional loading, the new formulation completely eliminates the nonconservative prediction error of 11.3%.

4 Concluding remarks
The work presented in this thesis was inspired by engineering
challenges related to the structural assessment of metallic floating
photovoltaic structures. It aimed to develop generalizable knowledge
about sheet metal forming and high-cycle fatigue by addressing four
research questions.
The thesis discussed opportunities and challenges related to the floating
photovoltaic concept that inspired the research. The current sheet metal
forming-based design was introduced, and aspects related to the highcycle fatigue capacity of such structures were briefly addressed.
Objectives, research questions, and strategies were presented.
Section 2 introduced important theoretical concepts, and reviewed stateof-the-art analysis approaches with the purpose of identifying research
gaps. Extra attention was dedicated to continuum mechanics-based
fatigue modeling approaches, and previous work that combines sheet
metal forming and fatigue analyses.
The research gaps were targeted in five scientific publications, with their
contributions outlined in Section 3. A brief summary of each publication
was provided, highlighting their relevance to the specific research
questions.
Publication I studied the sheet metal forming of a repeated cup shape in
the floating photovoltaic structure. A parametrized numerical model was
used to study the relationship between two geometric parameters in LSDYNA: the cup depth, and the drawbead distance. For a given
parameter combination, the calibrated model predicted whether the
forming operation would turn out successful or not. An algorithm was
developed to run a series of analyses by varying the parameter values
based on the outcome. This was used to construct a failure limit diagram
in terms of the two parameters. The results showed that increased
drawbead distances allowed larger cup depths.
Publications II and III explored how the sheet metal forming process
influences the fatigue capacity of the floating photovoltaic floats. Results

64

Concluding remarks

from local sheet metal forming simulations were used in submodel
analyses to predict the operational stresses in the floating photovoltaic
structure. This involved developing a submodeling technique that could
accommodate for geometric imperfections caused by springback. The
results suggested that the fatigue capacity was compromised by
springback-induced geometric deviations and residual stresses.
Moreover, the results were sensitive to the parameter that controls the
isotropic-kinematic hardening formulation.
Although a simple load case was analyzed, Publication II demonstrated
the use of a continuum mechanics fatigue model to analyze fatigue in
the floating photovoltaic structure. This model treats arbitrary stress
histories in a unified manner and is thus appropriate for use in timedomain analysis of structures that experience complex non-periodic
loading. However, as the model assumes that the ratio between the
fatigue limits for fully reversed shear stress and normal stress has a
fixed value of 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 = 1⁄√3, the model cannot describe fatigue in very
ductile materials where 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 < 0.5, or brittle materials, where 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1
is significantly larger than 1.
Publications IV and V took a closer look at the continuum mechanics
fatigue model and its shortcomings for very ductile or brittle materials. In
a first attempt to rectify the model’s limitation, a Hershey-Hosford
function was adopted as the deviatoric part of the endurance surface.
This provided a solution for ductile materials with 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 -ratios ranging
from 1⁄2 to 0.585. To further extend the model to describe brittle fatigue
behavior, it was shown that the continuum mechanics fatigue model
complies with the second law of thermodynamics for non-convex
endurance surfaces. This enabled us to develop a formulation for
arbitrary 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 -ratios, providing a unified formulation for very ductile
and brittle materials. Both formulations were validated towards an
extensive amount of multiaxial fatigue data from experiments reported in
the literature.
Taken together, the thesis provides important scientific contributions to
the state-of-the-art of sheet metal forming and fatigue analysis. The

65

Concluding remarks

introduction of a submodeling technique that allows users to predict
operational stresses in formed components with improved realism have
enabled a better understanding of how the forming process influences
the fatigue capacity. Moreover, the generalizations of a continuum
mechanics fatigue model [22] have expanded its application domain to
very ductile and brittle materials.

Further work

5 Further work
Due to the restricted scope of this thesis, several related topics remain
open for further investigation. The author would like to emphasize the
importance of the following areas as promising directions for further
research:
• Obtaining realistic load scenarios for marine FPV structures:
Knowledge of realistic load histories is required for the presented
methodologies to be used as parts of comprehensive fatigue
assessment. Research should explore how the loads depend on
the matrix size, the properties of the connective hinges, and the
local metocean conditions.
• Experimental validation of sheet metal forming analyses:
The analyses obtained with calibrated sheet metal forming models
in LS-DYNA require experimental validation. Various plasticity
models’ ability to predict forming limits have been widely explored,
but little effort has been directed towards the accuracy of the
residual stress predictions. The present investigation employed a
hypoelastic formulation that is path-dependent [34]. It should be
examined whether a hyperelastic formulation is required to achieve
satisfactory accuracy in the subsequent fatigue analysis.
• The Portevin-Le Chatelier phenomenon and fatigue:
The AA5083-H111 demonstrated serrated stress-strain curves due
to the Portevin-Le Chatelier effect [160]. It should be investigated
whether the phenomenon introduces effects that should be
considered in the fatigue analysis of formed structures made from
materials where this effect is present.
• Strain hardening and its influence on fatigue:
Strain hardening can influence the fatigue capacity of formed
components. In this study, such effects were disregarded due to
poor experimental evidence about their impact on the fatigue
capacity. Future research should strive to better understand the

67

Further work

underlying mechanisms, and find ways to account for the effect in
fatigue analyses of formed components.
• Fatigue induced by out-of-phase bending and torsion:
More experimental data is needed to fully understand the effect of
the phase shift between cyclic loading in bending and torsion.
Future experimental campaigns should examine the phase-shifteffect for finite and infinite fatigue lifetimes. Moreover, materials
with various 𝜏−1 ⁄𝜎−1 -ratios should be tested.
• Out-of-phase bending and torsion in the continuum
mechanics fatigue model:
The behavior of the continuum mechanics fatigue model for out-ofphase combinations of bending and torsion is still ambiguous.
These issues should be thoroughly investigated and, if necessary,
modified formulations should be proposed.
