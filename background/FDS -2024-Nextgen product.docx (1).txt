3. Solutions
FP1 (produces power)
We use a high power chinese standard PV and its frame.
Risen

Panels are interconnected using standard MC4 connectors


FP2 (certified)
The PV panels we use are certified


FC1 (floats)
The PV is attached on a “float” that provides enough buoyancy for the PV to stay above water? Dip in water to take advantage of the water cooling ? 
We will use as much experience from our previous experiences that we can (Aluminum floating)
Keep in mind that we will have to walk across the matrix carrying anther solar panel, together with another person.
FC2 (Is watertight)
The PV and the float must resist water intrusion, We will be sealing the float with sealing technology that we have experience with. 
We will also protect the PV (cells + junction box) from water. 
FC3 (Is attached to other FPV)
Each individual FPV will be equipped with a set of connectors that will link the FPV unit together. 


FC4 (resist to current flow)
We should design a “head” FPV that will protect a string of FPV to dip into water when exposed to current flow


FS1 (Can walk on it)
The PV glass should be supported enough to enable a human to walk on it without breaking. Therefore, we need to set up a support  behind the PV glass if we want to walk on the PV.




4. Solutions
Hinges/Octopatch 

Hinder floats bumping into each other
Allow movement in all directions
Allow stretch and stop
Disallow snatch
Cost efficient
Interchangeable
Can be preassembled
Can be assembled at site
Resists wear and tear
UV radiation and heat resistant
Possible to add multiple at edges and fewer in the center[a][b]

Float 
Float joined by, for instance, roller hemming, glue, friction stir welding.
P-joint (also called teardrop or rope hem)
Allows for pinching hinge onto
Safe to touch
Neat looking
Cost efficient



Filled with foam
        Cannot sink
        Will disallow gas expansion (contained in mini bubbles)
        Allows for very thin Al, perhaps 0.7 mm.
        Very strong sandwich construction.
        Bonds very well to Al.
        PU foam is a well known marine material.
        Might be difficult, due to safety issues, used in norway.
        Poorer heat transfer, but better thermal mass.
        Less thermal cycling


Solar panels mechanically attached to the top of the float.
Ideally using CD weld studs and nuts.
        Probably using end clamps


Float/PV may have a few degrees angle for shedding off water (and bumping up power production?).


Honeycomb alu structure between glass and the bottom plate.
Will trap air inside each honeycomb. Can be alleviated with small channels between each honeycomb.
Challenging to fasten to glass and bottom plate.


The frame exposes the edge of the glass panel to water ingress. This can be minimized by i.e PU foam. Foam can be along the edge or outside of a honeycomb, polystyrene or fluid formed alu cups.


The float and panel sandwich will trap air. The air will expand and contract. We need to make sure this does not delaminate anything (ie glue or silicon) and that it does not cause fatigue in the solar panel. A potential solution is to let each float breathe via a narrow hole.
